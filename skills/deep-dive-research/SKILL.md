# Deep Dive Research

## Overview

Not every cognitive bias deserves deep study. You'll encounter mentions of dozens of biases throughout your life, but only a subset will be relevant to your specific decisions, domains, and challenges. The key is knowing when a 15-30 minute deep dive will pay dividends versus when a quick reference is sufficient.

Deep understanding transforms bias awareness from abstract knowledge into practical decision-making improvement. When you truly understand why a bias exists, how it manifests in your specific context, and what triggers it, you can catch it in real-time. This skill is about strategic learning: investing focused time on biases that matter to you.

The goal isn't encyclopedic knowledge of all 188 documented biases. Instead, develop deep expertise in the 5-8 biases most relevant to your work and life. This focused approach enables pattern recognition, effective mitigation, and ultimately better decisions where they count most.

## When to Do a Deep Dive

Invest 15-30 minutes in comprehensive research when:

- **Recurring pattern**: The bias keeps appearing in your decisions or outcomes
- **Domain-critical**: Working in a field where this bias is particularly dangerous (e.g., Halo Effect in hiring, Availability Bias in risk assessment)
- **High-stakes preparation**: Facing an important decision where this bias could be costly
- **Teaching others**: Need to explain the bias to your team or organization
- **Building systems**: Creating checklists or processes to counter biases systematically
- **Genuine curiosity**: Fascinated by the cognitive mechanism and want to understand deeply
- **Lollapalooza potential**: This bias combines with others you already struggle with

## When NOT to Deep Dive

Skip the comprehensive research when:

- **Single encounter**: Just read about the bias once in passing
- **Time pressure**: Need to make a decision now (use [[Quick Recognition]] instead)
- **Low relevance**: Bias doesn't apply to your typical decisions or domain
- **Already mitigating**: You're effectively managing this bias without deep knowledge
- **Completionist impulse**: Trying to "study them all" rather than focusing on what matters
- **Procrastination**: Using research as excuse to delay actual decision-making

## Comprehensive Research Process (25-30 minutes)

### Step 1: Read the Bias Note (5-7 minutes)

Start with the Tier 1 note if available; otherwise begin with Tier 2 and decide if deeper research is warranted.

Read all sections completely without skimming. Each section serves a specific purpose:
- **Overview**: Contextualizes the bias and its importance
- **Core Principles**: Explains the mechanism (how it works)
- **Evolutionary Function**: Reveals why we have this "bug" (often it was a feature)
- **Limitations**: Shows when the mental shortcut fails
- **Examples**: Demonstrates real-world manifestations across domains

Pay special attention to the evolutionary function section. Understanding that Availability Bias exists because rapid threat assessment was survival-critical helps you recognize it's not a character flaw, it's an outdated adaptation.

### Step 2: Connect to Your Context (5-7 minutes)

Abstract knowledge won't change behavior. You need personal connection.

**Identify 2-3 recent examples from your own experience:**
- What decision or judgment did you make?
- What was the trigger that activated the bias?
- What was the actual or potential cost?
- Could you have caught it in real-time with awareness?

**Write these down** in your decision journal or the documentation template below. The act of writing forces specificity and creates retrievable memory.

Ask yourself: "If I encounter this bias trigger next week, what will I notice differently?"

### Step 3: Explore Related Biases (3-5 minutes)

No bias operates in isolation. They cluster and compound.

Read the "Related Biases" section carefully, then skim 2-3 of those bias notes to understand:
- **How they cluster**: Anchoring + Adjustment Heuristic + Confirmation Bias often appear together
- **Compounding effects**: Multiple biases creating "lollapalooza effects" (Munger's term for when biases multiply damage)
- **Shared triggers**: Similar situations that activate multiple biases
- **Mitigation synergies**: Strategies that counter several related biases at once

Example: If studying Sunk Cost Fallacy, explore Loss Aversion and Status Quo Bias. All three make us stick with failing commitments.

### Step 4: Study Mitigation Strategies (5-7 minutes)

This is where understanding becomes action.

Read all mitigation strategies in the note, not just the first couple. Different strategies work for different people and situations.

**For each strategy, ask:**
- Could I actually implement this in my workflow?
- What would the trigger be to activate this technique?
- Do I need tools, templates, or accountability?

**Identify 2-3 strategies you can implement immediately.** Immediate means this week, not "someday."

Note which mental models counter this bias. Example: [[Inversion]] (thinking backwards) counters Confirmation Bias by forcing you to seek disconfirming evidence.

Create a personal implementation plan using the documentation template below.

### Step 5: Find Additional Resources (Optional, 5-10 minutes)

If this bias is truly critical to your domain, go deeper.

Check the "Resources & Further Reading" section:
- **Academic papers**: Reading abstracts is often sufficient for understanding methodology and key findings
- **Videos**: If you're a visual learner, 10-minute explainers can cement understanding
- **Books**: Note for future reading, don't try to read them now
- **Mental model notes**: Explore the models that counter this bias

Quality over quantity: One well-understood resource beats skimming five.

## Efficient Reading Strategy

Don't commit to 30 minutes immediately. Use progressive engagement:

**Skim first (3 minutes):**
- Read Overview section completely
- Scan Core Principles for main mechanism
- Read one example that matches your context

**Decide commitment:**
- Will 15 more minutes pay off for this bias?
- Is this actually relevant to decisions I face?
- Am I just procrastinating or genuinely learning?

**Deep read if yes (15-20 minutes):**
- Read all sections carefully, no skipping
- Pause to think about personal examples
- Take notes in your own words

**Take notes actively:**
- Don't just highlight, write your thoughts
- Rephrase concepts in your language
- Create your own examples

**Connect to existing knowledge:**
- Link to related mental models
- Reference past decisions where this appeared
- Note tools or systems that could help

## Progressive Depth Approach

Match research depth to your actual needs:

**Level 1 (2 minutes): Quick Awareness**
- Read Tier 2 note OR Overview section of Tier 1
- Understand definition and one example
- Know it exists and general mechanism
- **Use when**: Mentioned in article, low personal relevance

**Level 2 (10 minutes): Working Knowledge**
- Read full Tier 1 note, skim examples
- Understand core mechanism and when it appears
- Note 1-2 mitigation strategies
- **Use when**: Appears occasionally in your decisions

**Level 3 (20 minutes): Applied Understanding**
- Read Tier 1 note completely
- Write out 2-3 personal examples
- Choose mitigation strategies to implement
- Document in template above
- **Use when**: Recurring pattern in your decision-making

**Level 4 (30+ minutes): Deep Expertise**
- Read academic papers and books
- Create teaching materials or presentations
- Build systematic mitigation into workflows
- Teach others about this bias
- **Use when**: Domain-critical bias you need to master

## Building Personal Expertise

The goal is narrow depth, not broad shallowness.

**Focus on 5-8 biases most relevant to you:**
- What decisions do you make repeatedly?
- What domain are you working in?
- What biases appear in your decision journal?
- Which biases cost you money, time, or relationships?

**Deep dive each over 1-2 months:**
- Week 1: Initial research and documentation
- Week 2: Implement immediate mitigation actions
- Week 3-4: Practice pattern recognition
- Month 2: Refine strategies based on experience

**Revisit quarterly as situations arise:**
- When you catch the bias in real-time (success!)
- When you fall for it again (learning opportunity)
- When context changes (new job, new responsibilities)

**Teach others (best way to solidify):**
- Explain to colleague facing similar decisions
- Write internal documentation for your team
- Create checklist for specific decision type
- Share examples in decision retrospectives

Teaching forces clear thinking and reveals gaps in understanding.

## Success Metrics

You'll know this skill is working when:
- You catch biases in your own thinking before acting
- Can explain bias mechanisms to others clearly
- Have implemented 2-3 mitigation strategies that actually work
- Recognize bias triggers in real-time
- Make different (better) decisions in previously problematic situations
- Teaching others helps them improve their decisions

Remember: The goal isn't to eliminate biases (impossible) but to recognize when they're leading you astray and have tools to counteract them when it matters.
